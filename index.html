Search

Introduction

There's a good chance you've used a computerized search algorithm sometime in your life. Thanks to search algorithms, you have the ability to search for friends on Facebook, look for that book you wanted on Amazon, or find your favorite movie on Netflix. In this example, we'll travel back to a time before Netflix or Hulu and use the process of searching for a physical copy of a movie. I'll explain the process of linear search through the example of looking through a personal DVD collection and I'll explain a binary search by walking through the steps of searching for a movie in a store such as Blockbuster.

Linear Search

Overview

To demonstrate the process of a linear search, let's pretend like we're searching through our personal DVD collection for the movie Peter Pan. Our movies are unordered and sitting sitting on a shelf.

Most likely, we would scan the shelf until we found the movie we're looking for. We might look through the movies one by one and ask ourselves "is this the movie I'm looking for?" If not we would move along to the next movie. If we found the movie we were looking for we would grab the movie and end the search. If we didn't find the movie, we might terminate the search once we reach the end of the shelf and thus arrive to the conclusion that we do not have the movie.

Binary Search

Overview

Let's suppose you could not find Peter Pan in your personal collection and you had to instead go to Blockbuster. This approach adds a level of complexity to the situation due to the fact that the volume of DVDs we are dealing with is significantly larger. We could use the same process as we did in the first situation by starting from one end of the store and searching through each movies one by one, however, this might take a while. Let's think about one major difference between our personal collection and Blockbuster - Blockbuster is sorted. The movies are categorized according to the genre and within each genre the movies are categorized alphabetically. This allows us to perform another search method that would be more effective than the linear search. This search method is called a binary search.

Next, let's talk about the process. For example, let's say we are looking for the movie Peter Pan. There's an animated section that takes up the back wall so we walk to the middle of it and see Monster's Inc. We know that Peter Pan comes after Monster's Inc alphabetically so we can automatically discard all the movies to the left of Monster's Inc. from our search. Our search area is now narrowed down to where we are standing (Monster's Inc) to the end of the shelf (Up). We walk to the middle of our new search area and find Star Wars. We know Peter Pan would come before Star Wars so we can now discard all of the movies after Star Wars, narrowing down our search area even further. We now look to the right of Monster's Inc and to the left of Star Wars and fine Peter Pan, the movie we're looking for! Now let's try and translate this process to computer terms.

The Middle Loop

Lets now think about where we want to start our search. We might be tempted to start at the beginning if the movie we are looking for starts with an A or B or start at the end if the movie start with a Y or Z but what if we are looking for Bambi and all the movies before are Anastasia? By making our starting point the middle of the array we can guaruntee that we will be able to elimate half of the movies.

Applying Logic

Now that our function is organized, let's talk about the process when we are physically looking for the movie in Blockbuster. We already determined that starting in the middle would be the best choice, so lets go ahead and walk up to Monster's Inc. and consider the logic involved.

Condition #1: middle = target

Suppose that Monster's Inc was the movie we were looking for. It wouldn't make much sense to continue our search. Instead we would want to grab the movie, or in computer terms return the movie. Let's create an if statement that demonstrates this case.

In this example we'll assume the movie we're looking for is Monster's Inc. In this case we would grab the movie and head to check out, ending our search. If the movie we were looking for was not Monster's Inc, we would move on to an additional if statement which we'll go over in the next sections.

Step 5: Create an if statement for if the middle index is equal to the target. If it is then terminate the search and return the movie.

    var movies = ["captain america", "despicable me",
    ghostbusters", "harry potter", "monsters inc",
    peter pan", "star wars", "toy story", "up"];

    var target = "monsters inc";

    function binarySearch() {
      var start = 0;
      var end = movies.length;

      while (start < end) {
        var middle = Math.floor(start + end/ 2);
        if (movies[middle] === target) {
          return movies[middle];
        }
      }
    }

Condition #2: Target > Middle

Lets go back to our Peter Pan quest. This time when we walk up to our middle item Monster's Inc. it is not the movie we are looking for. So now what? Since Peter Pan comes after Monster's Inc alphabeticaly, we know that it will not be before Monster's inc. We can therefore eliminate all of the movies before Monster's Inc. Our new search area is now the movie after Monster's Inc to the end of the shelf. Since our beginning point changed, we now have a new middle.

In our code we need to create an if statement for the condition of the middle being greater than the target. If this is the case we shorten the search area by declaring the new start of the search to be the old middle and calculating a new middle.

Step 6: For the 2nd if statement, if the target is greater than the middle index, eliminate the first half of the search area by making the beginning the item after the old middle and calculate a new middle index

    var movies = ["captain america", "despicable me",
    ghostbusters", "harry potter", "monsters inc",
    peter pan", "star wars", "toy story", "up"];

    var target = "peter pan";

    function binarySearch() {
      var start = 0;
      var end = movies.length;

      while (start < end) {
        var middle = Math.floor(start + end/ 2);
        if (movies[middle] === target) {
          return movies[middle];
        else if (target > movies[middle]) {
          start = middle + 1; 
        }
      }
    }

Condition #3: Target < Middle

Let's now look at our new search area and look at our new middle item, Star Wars. Alphabetically, Peter Pan comes before Star Wars so now we need to eliminate all of the movies to the right of Star Wars. We can now say that our new end is Peter Pan (the old middle index number minus 1). Since Peter Pan is now both the beginning and the end, when we calculate the middle, (the index number of the beg (Peter Pan) + the index number of the end (Peter Pan) divided by two), Peter Pan also ends up being the middle. Now when we perform our final loop, Peter Pan is equal to the middle and therefore returned as the solution.

Step 7: For the 3nd if statement, if the target is less than the middle index, eliminate the second half of the search area by making the beginning the item before the old middle and calculate a new middle index

    var movies = ["captain america", "despicable me",

Condition #4: Movie Not Found

Just as in the linear search, we need to create a return statement in the case of the movie is not found. To demonstrate, instead of searching for Peter Pan, let's search for a movie not in the list - Snow White. We'll skip to the step where we have one movie left - the beginning, middle and end of the search area is Peter Pan. Unlike the last step, where we broke out of the loop and returned Peter Pan since our target was found, we will go through one more loop.

Since we know Snow White comes after Peter Pan and before Star Wars yet there is no movie there, we end our search. Let's take a look at what happens in the code. At this point Peter Pan is our beginning, middle and end of our search area since it is the only movie in our search area. When we make the final loop, since Snow White comes after Peter Pan alphabetically and is therefore greater than Peter Pan our new beginning is the middle + 1, which would be Star Wars, making our beginning greater than our end (Peter Pan). Since our while loop only runs when the beginning is less than the end, we would exit out of it and return not found
